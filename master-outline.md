# The Winter Lodge - Master Outline

## CHAPTER 1: ARRIVAL
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Owen drives his crew up the mountain road to Timberline Lodge for the final winterization before sale. The crew consists of his ex-wife Angie (chef), Patricia (bookkeeper), Donnie (maintenance), and Mark (documentary filmmaker). Tension immediately surfaces—Angie makes cutting remarks about Owen selling his "precious legacy," Patricia focuses on financial inventory checklist, Donnie grumbles about the weather forecast, Mark films everything despite complaints. The lodge appears massive and desolate, empty windows like dead eyes. They unload supplies and begin initial walkthrough. Owen shows Mark the layout while explaining the lodge's history (built 1951, family-owned, now bankrupt). First night preparation: assign rooms, fire up heating systems, establish communication (CB radio, landline phone). Mark interviews Owen about "final days of American dream," getting melancholic footage of empty lobby. Chapter ends with everyone settling into separate rooms, unaware they're being watched from the walls.

**Tone:** Melancholic nostalgia, establishing isolation and character dynamics

---

## CHAPTER 2: WINTERIZING
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Work begins. Donnie and Owen drain pipes in guest wings, board windows, shut down unnecessary systems. Patricia inventories furniture and kitchen equipment for auction. Angie preps the industrial kitchen for shutdown, sorting through walk-in freezer. Mark films the process, capturing "death of a business." Tension between Owen and Angie escalates—she confronts him about avoiding her, he deflects. Weather report on AM radio: major storm system developing, arriving 36 hours earlier than predicted. Donnie insists they can finish in time if they work faster. Patricia discovers discrepancies in financial records (equipment replaced multiple times, unexplained maintenance costs). First subtle wrongness: Angie finds food missing from locked pantry, assumes Donnie took it. Tools go missing from Donnie's workstation. Mark's cassette recorder picks up faint sounds he didn't notice during filming—breathing, movement. Nobody connects these details yet. Chapter ends with decision to work through the night to beat the storm.

**Tone:** Increasing urgency, first hints of wrongness beneath mundane tasks

---

## CHAPTER 3: TRAPPED
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Storm hits during night, far more intense than forecast. By dawn, three feet of snow has fallen, wind howling at 50mph, whiteout conditions. Vehicles buried. Road impassable. Owen tries the phone—line dead. CB radio works but ranger station reports road won't be cleared for minimum five days, possibly longer given conditions. Group realizes they're trapped with limited supplies (planned for three days, now facing week-plus). Immediate concerns: rationing food, conserving heating fuel, staying warm. Tension spikes—Angie blames Owen for not evacuating earlier, Donnie curses the weather, Patricia calculates resource depletion rates. Mark treats it as documentary gold, filming everyone's reactions, which annoys them. Owen takes charge: assigns shifts for heating system monitoring, establishes emergency protocols, inventories supplies. They secure the building against the storm. Strange detail: during supply check, they find a room key missing from the office board—Room 9. Nobody remembers taking it. Chapter ends with first night of entombment, wind screaming outside, darkness pressing in.

**Tone:** Escalating claustrophobia, survival concerns overtake interpersonal drama

## CHAPTER 4: ROOM 9
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Second day trapped. Owen and Patricia check guest wings to ensure no storm damage. In the east wing, they notice Door 9 is closed—all other rooms were left open for winterization. Owen doesn't remember closing it. They open it and find her: elderly woman, maybe 70s, lying on the bed, frostbitten, hypothermic, barely conscious. She shouldn't exist—all doors were locked, lodge was empty, they checked every room. Panic. They carry her to the lobby fireplace, warm her carefully. Angie provides blankets and hot water. Woman drifts in and out of consciousness, mumbling incoherently. Patricia checks the guest registry from the office—no record of anyone checking in. Donnie searches for how she entered—all exterior doors were locked from inside, no broken windows, no footprints in snow outside Room 9's window. Mark films her cautiously, documenting the impossibility. Woman's first coherent words, eyes wide with terror: "He's still here... he never left... 1952..." Then she lapses back into delirium. Chapter ends with group staring at each other, spooked and baffled.

**Tone:** Mystery transforms to genuine dread, the impossible made real

## CHAPTER 5: THE KEEPER
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Group debates explanations while caring for the woman. Angie thinks she's a squatter who hid when they arrived. Patricia points out the locked doors. Donnie suggests she's been here longer—maybe hid during final guest season. Owen doesn't recognize her but something nags at him. Mark researches in old lodge brochures, finds reference to local legend: "The Keeper," a mountain spirit that protects empty places, sometimes traps trespassers. Donnie scoffs, but Mark points out the woman keeps mentioning 1952. Patricia finds 1952 incident reports—guest disappeared during a storm, never found. The woman wakes briefly, lucid enough to grab Owen's hand: "Harold... he's in the walls... watching us..." Then incoherent again. Owen freezes—Harold was his uncle's name. First night sound: footsteps in the hallway above them. Everyone hears it. Donnie investigates with flashlight—hallway empty, but fresh wet footprints lead to a wall panel. When he touches it, it's loose. Behind: darkness. Chapter ends with Donnie slamming panel shut, shaken.

**Tone:** Folklore versus reality, dawning realization they're not alone

## CHAPTER 6: FIRST NIGHT
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Nobody sleeps. They stay together in the lobby, fire burning, woman unconscious near the hearth. Owen asks Donnie about wall panels—are there maintenance passages? Donnie admits there are small crawlspaces for pipes and wiring, but nothing a person could navigate extensively. Mark wants to explore immediately; others refuse. They establish watch shifts. During Patricia's 2 AM shift, she hears it: movement in the walls, scraping, then soft footsteps on the floor above. She wakes Owen. They listen together—definite footsteps, pacing, then stopping directly above them. Owen shouts "Who's there?" Silence. Then, faintly, through the ceiling: high-pitched giggle. Everyone wakes. Donnie grabs tools, Owen a fireplace poker. They search second floor—nothing, but doors they'd locked are now open. Mark's film equipment has been moved, cassette tapes scattered. In the kitchen, Angie discovers the walk-in freezer door ajar—food is missing, specific items: canned goods, jerky, crackers. Someone is living here. Chapter ends with them barricading in the lobby, realizing the storm outside is less dangerous than what's inside.

**Tone:** Primal fear, safe space violated, prey awareness

## CHAPTER 7: EVIDENCE
**Act:** 1
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Morning brings no relief. Storm still raging. Group commits: they must find whoever is in the lodge. Owen retrieves his father's old files from the office—documents about the lodge's history. Patricia assists, cross-referencing financial records. They discover pattern: incidents (fires, equipment failures, guest injuries) date back to 1960, suspiciously regular. Mark sets up his camera to record hallways continuously—maybe catch footage. Donnie begins systematic search of wall panels, finding multiple loose access points. First major discovery: behind panel in storage closet, a false ceiling. Donnie climbs through—attic crawlspace, large enough to move through. Evidence of habitation: old blankets, empty cans, smell of human occupation. They've found where someone lives. But the person isn't there. While searching, Angie stays with the woman, who wakes and speaks clearly for first time: "He lured me here... he said he had proof... about what they did to him..." Who lured her? "Harold. My brother. They said he died in 1972, but he wrote to me..." Chapter ends: Owen realizes the saboteur, the hidden occupant, the legend—it's his uncle Harold, alive and living in the walls for eight years.

**Tone:** Mystery pivots from supernatural to human horror; point of no return

## CHAPTER 8: PATRICIA'S DISCOVERY
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Patricia dives into financial records with new context. If Harold has been sabotaging the lodge since 1960, evidence should exist. She finds it: pattern of incidents aligns with family milestones—Harold's buyout (1960), Owen's father's wedding (1963), Owen's birth (1968), Harold's presumed death anniversary (every year since 1972). Sixty-three documented incidents over twenty years. Insurance claims total hundreds of thousands—enough to bankrupt the family slowly. She presents findings to Owen. He's devastated—his father spent his life fighting sabotage, died thinking he was cursed or incompetent, never knowing it was his brother destroying him. Angie sees Owen's anguish, her anger softens momentarily. Donnie remains skeptical—how could Harold survive in the walls for eight years? Patricia calculates: if he stole food regularly, used lodge utilities, raided supplies, it's possible. Mark asks the hard question: if Harold's been here since 1972, what happened to the three guests who disappeared in the 1970s? The woman (Harold's sister, name: Eleanor) wakes again, confirms: Harold contacted her six months ago, claimed he had proof the family stole his legacy, begged her to come see. She came. He locked her in Room 9 days ago, said she "betrayed him too." Chapter ends: they're trapped in a building with a man who's killed before.

**Tone:** Anger and grief; human villain emerges, more terrifying than ghost

## CHAPTER 9: HAROLD'S HISTORY
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Owen recounts what he knows about Uncle Harold. Brilliant but unstable, designed Timberline's infrastructure, oversaw construction. Partnership with Owen's father fell apart—Harold had vision but no capital, father had money but needed Harold's expertise. By 1960, Harold's erratic behavior (paranoia, accusations, volatile moods) led family to intervene. Declared incompetent, bought out, cut off. Harold fought legally, lost, disappeared into obscurity. Last seen 1972 during confrontation at the lodge—Owen was four, barely remembers a screaming man. Assumed Harold wandered into mountains and died. Patricia finds Harold's commitment hearing documents in old files—psychiatrist noted "delusional attachment to the building, refers to it as living entity." Donnie explores more crawlspaces, maps them—extensive network, Harold could move through entire lodge unseen. Mark reviews his footage from previous days, finds him: gaunt figure in background of three separate shots, watching from doorways, vanishing when Mark turns. Same person each time—wild hair, layered clothing, skeletal. They've been observed since arrival. Owen asks Eleanor: why now? Why escalate after years? Eleanor explains Harold told her this was the "end," the lodge was being sold, his creation destroyed. He wanted witnesses to his "justice." Chapter ends: Harold isn't just surviving; he has a plan.

**Tone:** Investigative momentum; villain's psychology comes into focus

## CHAPTER 10: RED HERRING
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Donnie proposes alternative theory: maybe Harold did die in 1972, and they're dealing with copycat—former staff member or competitor sabotaging the sale. He argues the financial pattern could indicate multiple actors over decades. Patricia counters with handwriting analysis: maintenance logs from 1960s match recent vandalism found in crawlspaces—same person. Angie supports Donnie's skepticism; accepting Harold is alive means accepting eight years of him living like a rat. Mark sides with evidence—the footage doesn't lie, someone elderly is here. Owen is torn; part of him hopes it's not Harold, that his uncle died peacefully rather than descending to this. They decide to search Harold's last known residence—a storage unit in town where his possessions were kept after 1972. But they're trapped by storm. Patricia suggests searching lodge office for anything Harold left behind. They find it: a box labeled "H. Reeves - Personal Effects 1972." Inside: journals, architectural drawings of the lodge with hidden passages marked, photographs of Harold's family with faces scratched out, letters to Owen's father begging for reconciliation (unsent). Final journal entry, dated 1972: "They'll never take her from me. The lodge is mine. I built her. I'll die in her walls before I let them have her." Chapter ends: It's Harold, and he's claimed the lodge as his forever.

**Tone:** Brief hope dashed; evidence mounts, escape from truth impossible

## CHAPTER 11: THE PASSAGES
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Donnie, Owen, and Mark systematically explore the hidden passages using Harold's architectural drawings as guide. The network is extensive: behind walls, under floors, through attic crawlspaces, connecting all three floors. Passages are cramped, dark, filled with pipes and wiring—navigable for someone who knows them, death-trap for others. They find caches: stored food (some decades old), water containers, kerosene heater, blankets, tools. Harold has been living like this for years. Disturbing discoveries: peepholes drilled through walls into guest rooms, allowing observation. A listening post near the office with primitive sound amplification (metal bowls against walls). Harold has watched and listened to guests, staff, family for decades. Mark films everything, nauseated. Owen finds sleeping pallet in attic crawlspace above east wing—Harold's primary nest, but he's not there now. Evidence suggests he moved recently. Where is he? They hear scraping behind them in the passage. Flashlights swivel—nothing visible, but sound of retreat, fast movement away. Harold knows they've found his home. Donnie notices something: the passages have been modified recently—new panels cut, routes expanded. Harold's been preparing. For what? Chapter ends with them exiting passages, shaken, realizing Harold has every advantage in this building.

**Tone:** Violating the predator's den; growing dread of his omniscience

## CHAPTER 12: MARK'S FOOTAGE
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Mark develops his film (portable kit for field work) and reviews all footage chronologically on his portable viewer. The group watches together. Harold appears in background of multiple shots over several days: standing in distant doorway during arrival (Chapter 1), visible through window reflection during winterization (Chapter 2), shadow moving in upper hallway (Chapter 3). Always watching, never approaching. But one shot is different: during the night before they found Eleanor (Chapter 3), Mark left his camera running in the lobby. At 3:47 AM, Harold enters frame. He approaches Mark's sleeping form, stands over him for ninety seconds, completely still, just staring. Then he reaches out, touches Mark's camera almost tenderly, and retreats into shadows. Mark goes pale—Harold could have killed him. Why didn't he? Patricia theorizes Harold is territorial but not immediately murderous; he was assessing threats. Owen wonders if Harold recognized him, knows Owen is family. Angie asks the vital question: if he's been watching us for days, why reveal himself now through the woman? Mark rewinds to earliest footage, finds answer: on arrival day, Mark filmed Owen saying "last trip, we're selling to developers, whole thing gets demolished in spring." Harold heard. This isn't defense—it's endgame. Chapter ends: Harold isn't trying to scare them away; he's preparing to die with his lodge, taking them with him.

**Tone:** Sick realization; retrospective horror of surveillance

## CHAPTER 13: ELEANOR'S TESTIMONY
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Eleanor is lucid enough to tell her story. Six months ago, she received letter from Harold—she'd believed him dead since 1972. Letter claimed he had proof their brother (Owen's father) forged documents to steal the lodge, that Harold deserved restitution. She initially dismissed it but Harold sent photos: himself alive, holding recent newspapers, standing inside the lodge. Shocked, she wrote back. Harold insisted she come see evidence in person, wouldn't trust mail or phone. She arrived three days before the crew, staying at a motel in town. Harold contacted her via motel phone, gave her instructions: enter lodge through basement window (he'd unlocked it), wait in Room 9. She complied, curious and concerned for her brother. Harold appeared that night—gaunt, wild, terrifying but lucid. He showed her the architectural drawings, explained his years of sabotage as "justice," begged her to witness his "final act" and tell the world the truth. She realized he was insane, tried to leave. He locked her in Room 9, took her car keys, said she'd understand once the "cleansing" happened. She's been trapped for three days, no food, freezing. Owen asks what "cleansing" means. Eleanor begins crying: Harold spoke of fire, purification, returning the lodge to "pure state before they corrupted her." Chapter ends: Harold plans to burn down Timberline with all of them inside.

**Tone:** Confirmation of worst fears; ticking clock starts

## CHAPTER 14: FIRST SABOTAGE

**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Harold strikes directly for the first time. Mid-afternoon, power goes out—not storm damage, deliberate. Donnie investigates basement electrical room, finds main breaker manually thrown and access panel removed, wiring damaged. While repairing, he hears movement in walls around him, rapid scuttling. Harold is close, watching. Donnie works frantically, gets partial power restored to essential systems. Returns upstairs to find more damage: CB radio smashed, antenna cable cut. Their only communication with outside world is gone. Angie discovers small fire in linen closet—set deliberately with kerosene, but burned out quickly. Warning shot. Mark's camera equipment is destroyed, film exposed to light, cassette tapes unwound. Harold is systematically eliminating their resources. Owen tries to organize defense, but Angie explodes—they should break through the snow, hike to town, take their chances with the blizzard rather than wait for Harold to pick them off. Owen argues it's suicide in whiteout conditions. Donnie supports Owen; they need to fortify, defend the lobby. Patricia calculates: if they barricade in one room, protect supplies, they can outlast Harold until rescue arrives in 4-5 days. Chapter ends with group barricading lobby entrance, Eleanor resting by fire, waiting for Harold's next move.

**Tone:** Open warfare begins; desperation tactics, primal siege mentality

Patricia inventories remaining supplies—enough food for five days if rationed, water from melted snow, firewood critical (if they lose heat, they die). Harold's attacks intensify. He cuts water pipes in basement—flooding begins. Donnie must venture out to shut off main valve, armed with pipe wrench as weapon. Owen accompanies him with flashlight. As they retreat, they see him: Harold stands at far end of basement, illuminated briefly by Owen's flashlight. Skeletal, wild-haired, wearing rotting layers, he stares at them with yellowed eyes. Owen tries speaking; Harold hisses and vanishes into shadows. They flee upstairs. That night, Harold pounds on walls around lobby—rhythmic, maddening, for hours. Nobody sleeps. He's wearing them down psychologically. Mark notices Angie is sharpening kitchen knives obsessively, talking to herself; stress is fracturing her. Patricia hasn't slept in two days, calculating survival odds constantly. Donnie is exhausted from constant repairs. Owen feels responsible for everyone. Eleanor barely speaks, traumatized. Harold knows they're weakening. Next morning, they find message scratched into lobby door (he did this while they slept feet away): "THE LODGE IS MINE." They check barricades—one panel was partially removed and replaced, meaning Harold was inside the lobby during the night, watching them sleep. Chapter ends: nowhere is safe.

**Tone:** Psychological warfare, sanity fraying, terror of violated sanctuary

## CHAPTER 18: THE LAIR
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Donnie proposes offensive plan: find Harold's main nest, destroy his supplies, flush him out. Owen agrees—they can't just defend forever. Using architectural drawings, they identify likely location: attic crawlspace above east wing, largest hidden space. Donnie, Owen, and Mark go (Angie and Patricia stay with Eleanor). They access crawlspace through storage closet. Inside, they find Harold's true lair. Sleeping pallet made of stolen guest linens. Kerosene heater (fire hazard, explains past lodge fires). Food cache spanning years—cans from 1960s mixed with recently stolen supplies. Disturbing shrine: photos of Owen's father with eyes scratched out, photos of Owen as child (taken without family knowledge), Eleanor's photo with "TRAITOR" written across it. The walls are covered in Harold's handwriting: "THE LODGE IS MINE" repeated thousands of times, obsessive spirals. Architectural plans with passages marked. And most chilling: photos of guests over decades, red X's marked over faces of three individuals—the missing guests from the 1970s. Evidence suggesting Harold murdered them (bloodstained clothing, personal effects). They're standing in a serial killer's den. Mark finds Harold's journal—recent entries detail plan to burn lodge using kerosene and propane, set fires in multiple locations simultaneously, "purify her and send her souls to heaven together." Chapter ends: Harold's endgame is tonight.

**Tone:** Horror of discovery; intimate glimpse into decades of madness

## CHAPTER 19: FIRE WARNING
**Act:** 2
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

They return to lobby with evidence. The journal makes Harold's plan clear: he's stockpiled accelerants throughout the lodge, plans to ignite them simultaneously, creating inferno too large to stop. The building is wood and old; it'll burn fast. They have hours at most before he acts. Debate erupts—Angie insists they brave the blizzard immediately. Patricia checks conditions: storm still raging, visibility zero, temperature below zero with wind chill. They'd freeze or get lost within minutes. Donnie suggests they hunt Harold down, stop him before he lights fires. Owen counters that Harold knows every hiding spot; they'll never find him in time. Mark proposes sabotaging Harold's plan—find the accelerants, remove them. But the lodge is massive; they can't search everywhere quickly. Eleanor offers insight: Harold always acted at night, darkness is his element. They have until nightfall. Patricia calculates: if they secure main exits, create clear escape route through snow, prepare emergency supplies, they can evacuate the moment fire starts and survive outside long enough for the blaze to signal rescue (fire visible for miles). Owen hates sacrificing the lodge but accepts it's necessary. They begin preparations: pack emergency supplies (food, blankets, kerosene heater), dig escape path through snow to nearby equipment shed (shelter), create fire-break by opening windows (counterintuitive but allows controlled burn versus explosive). Chapter ends: they're ready to flee, waiting for Harold's move.

**Tone:** Urgent preparation; acceptance of losing the lodge to save lives

## CHAPTER 24: RESCUE
**Act:** 3
**Word Count Target:** 1,700

Morning. Storm has passed. Group survived night in equipment shed using emergency supplies, kerosene heater, huddled together. Timberline Lodge is smoldering ruins—stone shell remains, everything else ash. Angie's wounds are serious but not life-threatening; Owen bandaged her with first aid kit. Eleanor is stable. Exhaustion and shock settle in. Mark looks at his ruined camera, notebooks intact. Patricia stares at financial records (she grabbed them during evacuation)—decades of sabotage documented, now moot. Donnie is silent, processing. Owen watches his family legacy burn to foundation. Around noon, they hear engines—snowplows clearing road, then rescue vehicles. Sheriff, paramedics, fire department arrive. Group is evacuated to ambulances. Sheriff asks what happened. Owen explains: early storm trapped them, electrical fire started (doesn't mention Harold yet—too complex). At hospital in town, they're treated for smoke inhalation, minor injuries, exposure. ## CHAPTER 30: LINGERING QUESTIONS

Winter 1981. Mark sends Owen package: final materials from documentary—unused footage analysis, audio enhancements. Included: enhanced audio from Chapter 20's recordings. Final image: Owen burns the audio recordings, choosing not to know. Some questions are better left unanswered. Lodge is gone, Harold is dead, they survived. That's enough.
